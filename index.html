University of Maryland — DARPA Triage Challenge
Robotics Engineer
+

Working on the autonomy stack for a legged robot deployed in MASCAL (Mass Casualty) scenarios — outdoor, unstructured environments where the robot must autonomously navigate, detect, approach, and assess injured casualties without any human intervention during a run. The platform carries a custom-built multi-modal sensor payload including RGB-D, thermal, Time-of-Flight, and radar sensors, all integrated and calibrated to feed both classical and learning-based perception pipelines.

My work on perception and autonomy covers the full detection pipeline: I develop and test ML models for person detection operating across RGB, thermal, and intensity data modalities, enabling the robot to reliably localize casualties in diverse lighting and weather conditions. Detected casualties are projected into the world frame for spatial localization. On top of detection, I run Vision-Language Models (VLMs) on edge hardware on the robot, combining visual data and contextual text to make automated assessments of injury patterns — estimating injury type and severity directly on-platform in the field. I also worked on a custom team-built drone, integrating GStreamer video streams and testing thermal person detection models for aerial support in the same scenarios.

I built a Qt-based operator GUI for situational awareness during mission runs. The interface displays live casualty locations on a map, thumbnail images and injury pattern summaries from each detected casualty, robot status indicators, and live trajectory visualizations. I also integrated an ATAK-based tablet application to push this information directly to medics in the field, giving first responders real-time situational awareness of casualty locations and assessed injury states before they reach the scene.

University of Delaware — Aqua2 Marine Robotics
Graduate Research Engineer
+

Worked on the Aqua2 autonomous underwater vehicle — a six-flipper AUV developed by Independent Robotics, designed for marine research in challenging open-water environments. Developed a nonlinear trajectory planner for the platform, validated first in a Unity-based simulation environment before field deployment. Testing was conducted in controlled pools at the Lewis Campus in Delaware and extended to a freshwater lake in Barbados for real-world open-water trials.

On the state estimation side, I performed IMU and camera calibration using Kalibr and tested and validated OpenVINS visual-inertial odometry on the AUV, evaluating the accuracy and robustness of the VIO pipeline in underwater conditions. For the perception side of the project, I built a YOLOv10-based oyster detection pipeline, addressing the challenge of limited underwater training data by generating synthetic augmentation data using stable diffusion. The final model achieved 0.657 mAP@50 running on the AUV's edge hardware. This work resulted in two co-authored publications.

University of Delaware — KUKA LBR iiwa Manipulation
Graduate Project — 7-DOF Arm
+

Designed and implemented a sub-millimetre pick-and-place system on the KUKA LBR iiwa 7-DOF collaborative arm. A camera detects ArUco markers in the scene and uses OpenCV's solvePnP to compute full 6-DOF pose estimation of target objects. From there, I derived the robot's Forward Kinematics (FK) and Inverse Kinematics (IK) analytically and implemented Jacobian pseudoinverse for redundancy resolution, with singularity detection and joint-limit enforcement to ensure safe, stable execution.

Trajectory planning was done in MATLAB, where I experimented with and compared multiple trajectory profiles — trapezoidal velocity profiles, polynomial (cubic and quintic) trajectories — evaluating the smoothness, torque requirements, and execution accuracy of each. The final trajectories were executed via C++ on the physical hardware, closing the loop between simulation and real robot. The system achieved consistent sub-millimetre placement accuracy across repeated pick-and-place cycles.

University of Delaware — iRobot Create 3 Navigation
Graduate Project — Mobile Robot
+

Built a complete navigation and perception stack on the iRobot Create 3 platform with a custom sensor payload — an Intel RealSense depth camera and a 2D LiDAR mounted on the robot. In ROS2, I developed behaviours for reactive navigation: wall following using LiDAR range data, obstacle detection and avoidance using both LiDAR and depth sensing, and object detection using OpenCV colour segmentation on the RGB stream. Each capability was tested and iterated in real hardware runs on the lab floor.

The course capstone was a minesweeper challenge: navigate a cluttered environment, detect and drive to two sequential green target balls in order, while actively avoiding a blue obstacle that must not be hit. I solved this using OpenCV-based colour detection to identify and classify targets in the camera frame, LiDAR-informed path planning in ROS2 to approach targets while clearing the obstacle, and a finite state machine to manage the task sequencing — search, approach target 1, confirm, search and approach target 2 — across the full run. The robot completed the challenge autonomously with no human input.

Swift Robotics
Robotics Engineer

Swift Robotics builds and deploys autonomous service robots for hospitality and healthcare — serving food and beverages in restaurants, hotels, and cafes, and performing UV disinfection rounds in hospitals. The robots operate in semi-structured indoor environments alongside humans, requiring robust navigation, reliable localisation, and smooth human-aware motion. I joined as a robotics engineer and took ownership of the software architecture and algorithm selection for the platform.

My core work focused on the SLAM and navigation stack. I benchmarked Cartographer against RTAB-Map across a range of indoor environments — cafes, hotel corridors, hospital wards — evaluating map quality, loop closure reliability, and computational overhead on the robot's onboard hardware. I evaluated multiple motion planners for performance in tight, crowded spaces. Based on these results I drove hardware and sensor upgrades to improve localisation stability and navigation consistency in real-world deployments. I was directly involved in testing the robot in live deployment environments — restaurants during service, and hospital floors — iterating on behaviours to handle dynamic obstacles, narrow passages, and unpredictable human traffic.

Drishti Works
Robotics Engineer
+

Drishti Works developed autonomous mobile robots for outdoor surveillance, with a primary application in beach and coastal monitoring. The robots were built to patrol defined routes, detect anomalies, and relay data to operators — operating continuously in challenging outdoor conditions. I was part of the team building these robots from the ground up, with full hardware-software integration responsibility.

On the hardware side, I led sensor selection and integration — choosing and integrating LiDAR, Intel RealSense, and ZED stereo cameras for perception and navigation. Low-level hardware communication was handled through I2C and UART protocols. I designed and tuned the robot's PID controller for precise velocity and heading control. In parallel, I built Gazebo simulation environments complete with synthetic noise models — damping, friction, sensor noise — to enable meaningful sim-to-real testing before field deployment.

I developed a diagnostic framework that monitored sensor health and system state in the field, significantly reducing debugging time and unplanned downtime during outdoor deployments. The robots were updated remotely through an AWS-based OTA fleet update pipeline, allowing software pushes without physically accessing each unit. Real-world testing on beach terrain in varying weather conditions was a significant part of my work — iterating on controller tuning, sensor configurations, and navigation parameters based on actual field performance.

TMRT — The Marine Robotics Team
Team Captain → AUV Engineer
+

TMRT is a student-led team that designs, builds, and competes with autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs) in international competitions. The flagship event is the Singapore AUV Challenge, where teams deploy fully autonomous underwater robots to complete a timed mission — gate navigation, acoustics-based localisation, vision-based tasks — without any human input once submerged. In 2018 and 2019, TMRT competed and finished in the top 30 teams globally, placing us among the best university AUV teams in the world. I also represented this work as a TEDx speaker.

My journey on the team began in the marketing and outreach role — building the team's public presence and managing sponsorships — before transitioning into the technical side and eventually being elected Team Captain for two consecutive years. As captain, I managed a cross-functional team of 15+ members across mechanical, electronics, and software — running sprint planning, coordinating between hardware and software timelines, and driving integration testing in the lead-up to competition. On the technical side, I built the robot's custom PID controller for depth hold and heading control, worked on ROS and Gazebo simulation infrastructure for algorithm testing, and integrated the Pixhawk flight controller with ArduSub for low-level motor control. The sensor stack included pressure sensors for depth estimation and IMU magnetometer fusion for orientation and heading, giving the AUV full state estimation underwater. Compute ran on Jetson and Odroid boards depending on the generation of the platform.
